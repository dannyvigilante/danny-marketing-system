# Audience Segments — Passing Notes Through a Glass Wall

## Primary Audience: White Parents Raising Black Children

**Demographics:**
- Adoptive parents of Black or mixed-race children
- Primarily White, primarily female (mothers drive adoption research and parenting decisions)
- Age: 30-55
- Income: Middle to upper-middle (adoption is expensive)
- Education: College-educated (likely to seek out books as resources)

**Psychographics:**
- Love their children deeply but sense they're missing something
- Aware that race matters but unsure how to address it in their family
- May have received training during adoption process but found it insufficient
- Hungry for real-world perspectives, not academic theory
- Some defensiveness is present but so is genuine desire to do better
- Active in adoption communities (online forums, Facebook groups, support organizations)

**What draws them in:**
- The anchor story — a mother who rejected the book, then wished she'd had it 40 years ago
- The specificity — this isn't about race in general, it's about THEIR family structure
- Danny was the child they're raising — that perspective is rare and invaluable
- The book is positioned as useful, not accusatory

**What converts them:**
- Hearing from other adoptive parents who found it helpful
- Danny's warmth alongside his directness — he's not attacking them
- The braided format — personal stories make the systemic analysis accessible
- Recognizing their own family patterns in the book

**Barriers:**
- Fear of being indicted or shamed
- Comfort with the "love is enough" narrative
- Defensiveness when Whiteness is named as infrastructure
- The "indictment" sections may create friction (intentionally)

---

## Secondary Audience: White People Doing the Work

**Demographics:**
- Age: 25-60
- Educated, progressive-leaning but not necessarily activist
- Already reading about race but looking for deeper, structural analysis
- May or may not be in transracial families

**Psychographics:**
- Have read the introductory books (White Fragility, How to Be an Antiracist)
- Looking for something that goes deeper — systems, not just feelings
- Value lived experience combined with intellectual framework
- Tired of performative DEI content but still committed to understanding

**What draws them in:**
- Danny's systems-thinking approach — this isn't another "feel bad" book
- The braided essay format offers a new reading experience
- The lack of citations signals confidence, not carelessness
- The institutional analysis (military, courts, corporate America) covers ground other books don't

**What converts them:**
- The quality of the writing and thinking
- Seeing their own institutional experiences reflected
- The framework is transferable — they can use it to analyze their own contexts

---

## Tertiary Audience: Black and Queer People Navigating Institutional Whiteness

**Demographics:**
- Black transracial adoptees (small but passionate community)
- Black professionals in predominantly White institutions
- LGBTQ+ people of color navigating multiple institutional layers

**Psychographics:**
- Need to feel seen, not studied
- Recognize the patterns Danny describes from their own lives
- May share the book not for education but for validation
- This audience doesn't need the book to understand — they need the book to feel understood

**What draws them in:**
- Representation — Danny's specific intersections (Black, gay, adopted, veteran, formerly incarcerated) are rare in published work
- The unflinching honesty about institutions that claim inclusion
- The refusal to perform gratitude or resilience for White comfort

**What converts them:**
- Word of mouth within community
- Social media content that reflects their reality
- Danny's voice — they recognize it as authentic

---

## Anti-Audience

- **Performative allies** who want to post about reading it but not engage with the content
- **Academic gatekeepers** who will critique the lack of citations (they missed the point)
- **Adoption agencies** looking for feel-good content (this book doesn't provide that)
- **White comfort seekers** who want to be told they're doing fine
